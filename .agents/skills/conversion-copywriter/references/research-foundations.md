# Research Foundations

Use these principles when writing or rewriting persuasive copy. They are distilled from the linked sources below and adapted into practical rules.

## Core principles

### 1. Write for the reader, not for the brand deck
Start with the buyer's context:
- Who is this for?
- What problem are they trying to solve?
- What outcome do they want fast?

If the audience is not visible in the copy, the message usually becomes generic.

### 2. Clarity beats cleverness
A strong headline should help the reader understand what is being offered and why it matters. Clever phrasing is optional; clarity is not.

Apply this by:
- naming the offer early
- avoiding metaphor-heavy openings when the user needs a direct commercial message
- replacing abstract brand language with concrete outcomes

### 3. Lead with benefits, support with features
Features matter, but only after the reader sees the payoff. Translate capabilities into real-world outcomes.

Weak pattern:
- feature -> feature -> feature

Stronger pattern:
- problem -> benefit -> proof -> feature detail

### 4. Specificity creates trust
Specific copy feels more credible than inflated claims.

Prefer:
- named outcomes
- concrete constraints
- operational detail
- exact audience language
- real delivery terms

Avoid:
- empty superlatives
- broad claims with no evidence
- generic AI or startup filler

### 5. Scannability matters
Readers skim. Make the structure easy to process.

Use:
- short paragraphs
- strong first lines
- direct CTA labels
- compact lists when they reduce friction

### 6. Human tone wins over corporate varnish
Mailchimp's guidance consistently pushes toward natural, respectful, useful wording. Sound informed, not inflated.

Use:
- plain verbs
- conversational rhythm
- direct phrasing

Avoid:
- stiff marketing cliches
- pretending certainty you do not have
- trying to sound premium by becoming vague

### 7. Every persuasive claim needs a truth standard
Google's people-first guidance aligns well with conversion writing: help the person first, do not optimize for manipulation.

Before keeping any persuasive line, ask:
- Is this useful to the reader?
- Is it true as written?
- Could the user defend this claim with real evidence?

If not, rewrite it or remove it.

## Truthfulness guardrail
Use this simple hierarchy for proof:
1. Direct evidence: metrics, named result, testimonial, usage fact, delivery detail.
2. Operational evidence: process, response time, revision policy, scope clarity.
3. Honest positioning: if proof is unavailable, make the offer clearer instead of louder.

Never fabricate proof to make the copy convert harder.

## Practical copy rules
- Put the offer in the frame early.
- Make the first sentence earn attention with meaning, not noise.
- Replace adjectives with outcomes.
- Replace weak CTA labels with specific actions.
- If the copy could describe ten other businesses, it is not specific enough.
- If the proof is weak, write cleaner copy instead of more aggressive copy.
